Melissodes

The genus Melissodes Latreille is a common group of new-worldNorth, South, and Centeral America. long-horned bees in the tribe Eucerini (Apinae). The antenna of the males often appear to be quite long in comparison to their body lengths, giving them the name "long-horned bees". However, there are a few exceptions of bee species in the subgenus Melissodes that have varying antenna lengths. Characteristcs of the two sexes commonly differ in appearance, especially in antenna length and scopialElongated specialized hairs, often found on the hind legs, used for the distribution of pollen. hair density (LaBerge, 1956). Melissodes is the second largest genus in Eucerini the tribe with 129 described bees and 5 current subgenera. The majority of the bees live in the United States and Mexico, except all species of Ecplectica are native to South America (Wright, 2016). Melissodes bees are shown to have some importance in agricultural pollination, pollinating crops such as cotton (LaBerge, 1956), alfalfa (LaBerge, 1956), muskmelons (Winfree, et al., 2007), watermelon (Campbell et al., 2018; Winfree, et al., 2007), conola (O'Brien & Arathi, 2018), and coffee (Ngo et al., 2013). Given the genus has so many species and is prevalent, it is reasonable to assume that they play an important role pollinating many other plants in nature as well.

Description and identification

Melissodes are medium-sized, setaceous bees with males often having long antennae where the first flagellar segment is short in comparison to rest of the antenna length (Laberge, 1956). Both sexes differ from Svastra in lacking spatuloplumoseA type of branched hair found on specific bees, that have a feather-like structure, and a spatula-shaped tip. hairs on the basal pubescent band of the second tergumThe upper portion of an arthropod segment. (T2). Males and females also subtly differ from other Eucerini genera in tegularA sclerite covering the base of the forewing. shape, with the exception of Martinapis, although to see the difference, removal of setae is often necessary. Male Melissodes differ from those in Svastra as a result of the presence of a denticleSmall tooth-like projection. on either side of the pygidialA structure found on the last segment of a bee’s metasoma protruding past the end of the bee creating a point. If present, its found on the 7th dorsal segment on males, and 6th on females. plate located beneath the strong lateral arm of the gradulusAn abdominal line present in some bees that is formed by a groove or difference in height in between two regions. of the 7th dorsal segment (Mitchener, 2007). Common characteristics in the majority of Melissodes species are as follows: short or absent malar margins of both sexes, the propodeum of both sexes has a distinct dorsal face that never slopes downward, and the scopialElongated specialized hairs, often found on the hind legs, used for the distribution of pollen. hairs of females have at least one or two branches on either side, excluding one species in Apomelissodes (Laberge, 1956).
